The four main categories of ramen

Shoyu Ramen

Shoyu ramen is defined by its characteristic inclusion of shoyu, or soy sauce, in the broth. This is a varied ramen type, as the broth can be made from any type of base, from bones to vegetables and everything in between. The addition of soy sauce lends its trademark savory and salty taste.

Shio Ramen

Shio ramen takes a lighter route than the shoyu ramen by using salt, typically sea salt, as it's flavoring base. Because the job of the chef is to showcase the salt's delicate flavor profile, a lighter broth base is typically used. Chicken bones or vegetable bases are good options here, as they lend themselves to a lighter flavor.

Miso Ramen

Miso ramen is a favorite of Hokkaido, in northern Japan. It features miso paste as its main flavoring component. Miso paste is made from fermented soybeans that are then ground. Miso provides a complex, balanced taste that is usually paired with complementary, but lighter, base broths. This is so the taste of the miso can come to the forefront and build upon the broth for a unique experience.

Tonkotsu Ramen

Tonkotsu ramen is different from the other 3 in that it describes the base rather than the source of salt used to flavor the broth. Tonkotsu is made by boiling pork bones that have a lot of natural connective tissue for many hours, to break down and gelatinize that tissue which produces a thick, creamy broth that is often opaque and milky white in appearance. Tonkotsu can be flavored with anything, but soy sauce is most common.
